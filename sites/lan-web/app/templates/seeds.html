Strength of Schedule

FAIRNESS AUDIT — COMPUTED & VERIFIED
{# Publishes the ACTIVE draw only. The alternative draws and their scores are a staff decision and stay staff-side (see the is_admin Options panel) — never name them here, and never state this draw's merits by comparison with them. #}

Could Saturday's draw be any fairer? Command put the question to the machine. Every one of the 11,180,820 legal ways to draw this ten-company field — each team facing six of the other nine, no rematches, no top-four meeting before Round 4 — was enumerated and scored on the evenness of its schedules. A perfectly even field is provably out of reach: no draw in that space gives all ten companies an identical slate. The draw that was selected, and how it scores, is set out below.

The draw, measured

Mean slate strength is fixed at 33 for every possible draw — only the spread moves. The bar reads difficulty — a fuller bar means a tougher draw (facing lower, stronger seeds) — and is drawn against the full range of slates a company could have been dealt, so the bars sit close together because the slates genuinely are close together.

Method. Each captain ranked the other nine companies (1 = strongest); ballots are weighted by the ranking team's own standing and iterated to a fixed point, so stronger teams' opinions carry more weight (lower score = stronger = better seed). A team's strength of schedule is the sum of its six opponents' seed numbers. The audit scored that figure's spread across all ten teams for every legal draw — subject to the two standing rules, no rematches and no top-four meeting before Round 4 — and the draw was picked to hold that spread tight while keeping the games worth showing up for. Chasing the last of the spread means giving up marquee games, and the difference it would make is smaller than a single map can swing in a best-of-one.
